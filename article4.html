What is a TWINDEMIC?



During the COVID-19 pandemic, the terms twindemic and tripledemic are used to describe the possibility of a severe flu season occurring alongside an increase in COVID-19 cases in the fall and winter of 2020 and 2021, as well as respiratory syncytial virus in the winter of 2022.

The term 'Twindemic' refers to the simultaneous occurrence of the flu and the novel coronavirus. Although the pathogenesis of these viruses differs, a cooperative effect is thought to be induced, causing both flu and COVID-19 cases to rise concurrently and overwhelm healthcare resources.

When did the term 'Twindemic' appear?

The term 'Twindemic' first appeared in a New York Times article in August 2020. The term was coined by LJ Tan Ph.D., Chief Strategy Officer for the Immunisation Action Coalition, a non-profit organization dedicated to increasing vaccine uptake.

The importance of vaccinations in preventing a 'Twindemic'

In an effort to mitigate the effects of the 'Twindemic,' the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) in the United States updated flu guidelines. In conjunction with the revised guidelines, the introduction of the COVID-19 vaccine is viewed as a critical means of mitigating influenza and influenza-like illness outbreaks in the coming year.

The most significant change to the CDC immunization guidelines requires that all vaccines administered be quadrivalent, or capable of protecting against all four of the most common influenza viral strains. These include influenza A through D, to provide vaccine recipients with a higher level of protection than in previous years.

Several factors influence influenza immunity, including previous exposure to influenza, vaccination history, and the prevalence of the strain relative to commonly circulating strains in previous seasons. Influenza seasons vary in severity and length, and this is determined by the predominant viral strain as well as population immunity.

The impact of vaccinations on the 'Twindemic'

Vaccination coverage varies by country, but influenza vaccination is typically given to infants and those considered at risk (those who are immunocompromised, have chronic health conditions, are over the age of 50, are pregnant, or work in the healthcare sector). This year, vaccination coverage is expected to be more extensive, with vaccinations routinely recommended to anyone over the age of six months (as in the United States) who does not have any underlying comorbidities.

A clinical trial recently concluded that combining any vaccine with the COVID-19 vaccine or adenoviral vaccine is safe and produces immunogenicity at a level comparable to separate vaccinations. This then lends support to combination vaccines as a safe and effective intervention to avert a potential "Twindemic."

As a result, vaccination against influenza is a critical intervention for reducing the severity of acute respiratory illnesses and preventing associated morbidity and mortality.

The role of vaccine hesitancy in the 'Twindemic' risk

However, the World Health Organization has reported significant drops in the receipt of the COVID-19 vaccine, indicating vaccine hesitancy. The Strategic Advisory Group of Experts defines this as a "delay in acceptance or refusal of vaccination despite the availability of vaccination services."
Vaccine hesitancy has increased globally as vaccination rates have declined, leading to an increase in the outbreak of vaccine-preventable diseases.

As a result, urgent efforts are required to address vaccine hesitancy and improve vaccine acceptance among populations worldwide. Several recommendations include collaborating with researchers and local health workers to develop culturally sensitive vaccination education programs, creating and distributing vaccine educational material, using fact sheets to prevent misinformation and dispel myths about the COVID-19 and influenza vaccines, and ensuring that vaccines are equally accessible.
