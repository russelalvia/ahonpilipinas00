What is a Variant?



Viruses have the ability to change and alter their genetic make-up. When these changes take place, the virus may become more or less dangerous to the human host. As a result, virologists are interested in tracking the evolution of the viral genome. The SARS-CoV-2 virus is designated as a single species, but there are various types of 'coronavirus' that can infect the patient with the same disease. These various types of viruses have been dubbed mutants, variants, strains, and even subspecies, causing conceptual confusion. This article defines what we mean when we say a viral variant.

The COVID-19 pandemic and the term variant

Biologists use specific terminologies to describe viruses, and these terms have crept into common parlance. Although there are significant differences between viruses, they have been collectively referred to as mutants, variants, subspecies, and strains. The term 'variant' has only recently come into use.
Following the COVID-19 pandemic, the term has entered popular discourse. One reason for this could be the plethora of sub-disciplines and specializations within biology, with scientists working in different areas of expertise using different terms.
For example, in order to describe a distinct branch of the virus' evolution, evolutionary biologists and biological virologists can use the term lineage. This is mainly due to the respect for an evolved tree that has been specially adapted for its taxonomic purposes. Meanwhile, rather than visualize the branch of a tree, experts in public health would more likely use the term ‘variant of concern’ (VOC) for a disease-causing biological organism or entity.

It may then explain why the widespread use of this variant during the COVID19 epidemic occurred, given that global pandemics are indeed a public health issue. In general the term may prove especially helpful as it refers to both viruses and illnesses, whereas terms like viral strain are specifically employed for this purpose. As the SARCoV-2 virus is essentially a single kind, there are few instances where it would be necessary to call this strain by its own name. Thus, the use of a term such as "variant" can help reduce Technical lnaccuracy and eliminate any problem of Conceptual Inconsistency.

Delta is an excellent example of this variant. The Delta variant (B.1.617.2) was only very recently (Fall 2021) the most common type of coronavirus in the US. The ability to infect human cells was doubled in this virus comparative to other variants of the virus ––a superpower that has since been superseded by Omicron. In those who were infected, Delta was believed to cause more severe illnesses and this is not disputed by the experts.
