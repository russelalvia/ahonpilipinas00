The Impact of COVID-19 Pandemic on Education System


MANILA, 22 August - Millions of students returned to classrooms across the Philippines today, for the first time in more than two years. The Philippines was one of the last few countries to transition back to face-to-face learning after the COVID-19 pandemic disrupted education across the world.
Around 24,000 of the country's public schools - or just less than half - will resume five days of face-to-face classes this week. The rest will hold a mix of in-person and online classes until November 2nd, when all 27 million registered students are expected to head back to the classroom full-time.

The Impact of the Lockdown on Higher Education

Both public and private higher education institutions (HEIs) have had to adjust to the new situation, which prohibits face-to-face interaction and large-scale gatherings. The top universities and colleges in the Philippines, especially those that are part of the ASEAN University Network, such as the University of the Philippines, Ateneo de Manila University, and De la Salle University, came up with novel ways to carry out their three responsibilities of education, research, and community service. They were dedicated to carrying out their mandate. Work-from-home arrangements were accepted by all, including administrators and teachers as well as the operations and support service units.
As they switched to alternative or remote teaching modalities, both synchronous and asynchronous, teachers and administrators were tasked with revising and adapting course syllabi and requirements from the comfort of their homes. Learning management systems like Canvas, Moodle, and Blackboard, in addition to applications like Google Hangouts, Zoom, and Skype, were utilized wherever educators and students had access to electronic devices and dependable Internet connections. Be that as it may, where understudies had restricted admittance to PCs or untrustworthy admittance to the Web, instructors, and understudies utilized cell phones to trade messages, notes, and materials, through message informing, email, Facebook Courier, and Twitter.
Because of the varying socioeconomic status of students, these arrangements sometimes became unsustainable, necessitating the suspension of remote or online classes at some universities. Likewise, concerns concerning the emotional wellness of the two understudies and educators impacted by the vulnerability, turned into one more motivation to suspend online classes.

Education in the time of COVID-19

In all areas, the coronavirus disease (COVID-19) pandemic has brought about an unprecedented crisis. This emergency has resulted in the widespread closure of face-to-face activities at educational institutions in over 190 countries in order to mitigate the virus's impact and prevent its spread.
The Economic Commission for Latin America and the Caribbean (ECLAC) has argued that the social situation in the region was getting worse before the pandemic hit because of the persistence of inequality, rising rates of extreme poverty, and growing social discontent. The crisis will have a profoundly negative effect on employment, poverty, and the various social sectors, particularly education and health care. As a result of the crisis, the suspension of face-to-face classes at all levels has resulted in three main areas of action, according to UNESCO, which has identified major gaps in educational outcomes related to the unequal distribution of teachers in general and of the best-qualified teachers in particular. This is to the detriment of lower-income countries and regions as well as rural areas. the use of distance learning methods in a variety of formats and platforms (whether or not technology is used); the assistance of educators and communities, as well as their mobilization; what's more, regions, where native and transient populaces will generally be concentrated.
Many of the measures that the countries in the region have taken to improve education focus on students' health and overall well-being. The point of this report is to reveal insight into different results that these actions will have on instructive networks in the short and medium term, and to propose key suggestions on the most proficient method to deal with those outcomes in the most ideal way, causing to notice potential open doors for learning and advancement in the post-pandemic schooling system.
