The COVID-19 pandemic

A Brief History of the Coronavirus Outbreak

• Is one of the most dangerous challenges this world has faced in our lifetime. It is above all a human crisis with severe health and socio-economic consequences.
COVID-19 is caused by one virus, called severe acute respiratory syndrome coronavirus 2, or SARS-CoV-2.

• The World Health Organization (WHO) has named a novel coronavirus (CoV) '2019-nCoV' or '2019 novel coronavirus' or 'COVID-19' in charge of the current pneumonia outbreak, which began in early December 2019 near Wuhan City, Hubei Province, China.

• COVID-19 is a dangerous virus. Based on phylogenetic analysis of available full genome sequences, bats appear to be the COVID-19 virus reservoir, but the intermediate host(s) has yet to be identified. Despite the fact that three major areas of work are already underway in China to raise awareness of the pathogenic origin of the outbreak.

• These include early investigations into cases with symptoms in Wuhan during December 2019, ecological sampling from the Huanan Wholesale Seafood Market and other area markets, and the collection of detailed reports on the point of origin and type of wildlife species marketed on the Huanan market, as well as the destination of those animals after the market has closed.

OVERVIEW
Update on COVID-19 - 1 June 2023

Globally, nearly 2 million new cases and over 12 000 deaths were reported in the last 28 days (1 to 28 May 2023), a decrease of 30% and 39%, respectively, compared to the previous 28 days (3 to 30 April 2023). During this 28-day reporting period, 150 of 243 (62%) countries and territories reported at least one case. The situation is mixed at the regional level, with increases in reported cases seen in the Western Pacific Region and the African Region, and decreases in deaths in all six WHO regions. As of 28 May 2023, over 767 million confirmed cases and over 6.9 million deaths have been reported globally.

Stay Informed

It may be tempting to try to block out the world altogether to avoid bad news, but it’s important to keep yourself informed. We all have to step up during a pandemic because we all have a part to play in reducing the spread of the virus. It’s important that you know what must be done and how you should do it. This is important for the health of your neighbours and your own mental health—taking action can help counter difficult feelings like hopelessness and despair.
One study from China found that people who had accurate instructions on how they should behave (such as instructions around hand-washing and wearing a mask) and reliable, current information about the coronavirus and COVID-19 illness felt more resilient and felt better able to handle the virus. Low levels of stress, anxiety, and depression were reported by those who received reliable, accurate information.
Setting boundaries is certainly acceptable. You don't have to follow the news nonstop to stay informed. Follow dependable sources and media outlets when you check in throughout the day. While social media can be a great way to stay in touch with family and friends, it can also serve as a platform for spreading bad information, hazy or false rumors, and other unhelpful stories. Use sound critical thinking techniques at all times.
